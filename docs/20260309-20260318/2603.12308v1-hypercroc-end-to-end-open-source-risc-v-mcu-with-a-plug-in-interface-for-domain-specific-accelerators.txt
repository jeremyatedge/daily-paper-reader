Abstract 

Domain-Specific architectures with accelerators for machine learning and signal processing require efficient bulk data movement and high-bandwidth access to large datasets. Such capabilities are often absent from minimal open-source microcontrollers (MCUs). We present HyperCroc , an extension to the end-to-end open-source RISC-V Croc system-on-chip (SoC) integrating a silicon-proven HyperBus controller for off-chip DRAM and Flash memory access and a DMA engine, providing a practical MCU-class platform with streamlined plug-in support for domain-specific acceleration. HyperBus offers a low-pin-count PSDRAM interface at up to 400 MB/s ,enabling bandwidth-scaled dataset access, while the DMA engine enables autonomous, high-throughput transfers without CPU intervention. HyperCroc preserves Croc’s open-source synthesis and physical implementation flow targeting IHP’s open 130 nm process design kit (PDK); the full chip can be implemented in under one hour on a consumer-grade workstation. We further report first silicon measurements from MLEM , the first Croc tapeout, confirming that the silicon is fully functional at 72 MHz @ 1.2 V and validating the end-to-end flow. 

## Introduction 

Access to semiconductor technology, electronic design automation (EDA) tools and reusable intellectual prop-erty blocks is a prerequisite to educate new chip design-ers and turn research prototypes into credible hard-ware. In the context of worldwide “Chips Acts” [1, 2] and a persistent skill shortage, end-to-end open-source (OS) platforms are increasingly important to lower the barrier to entry by making the entire design flow ac-cessible. The OS RISC-V Croc system-on-chip (SoC) was developed in this spirit [3], pairing a minimal microcontroller (MCU) with a streamlined RTL-to-GDS flow targeting IHP’s open 130 nm process design kit (PDK), supported by documentation and lecture material with hands-on exercises. At the same time, many important accelerator and signal-processing workloads are dominated by bulk data movement and access to large datasets. Field-programmable gate array-based prototypes can ac-cess high-bandwidth memory, but may not reflect application-specific integrated circuit (ASIC) perfor-mance [4] and integration constraints [5]. Conversely, ASIC-oriented OS MCUs such as Croc omit external memory interfaces, limiting their applicability as an extensible platform for domain-specific accelerators. To address this gap, we present HyperCroc , an ex-tension of the Croc end-to-end OS RISC-V MCU that adds the memory infrastructure required to support domain-specific accelerator plug-ins. HyperCroc inte-grates a silicon-proven HyperBus controller [6], pro-viding a low-power and low-pin-count PSDRAM inter-

The Croc domain combines a performance oriented 3-stage Ibex core (RV32IMCB), peripherals, and a tightly coupled single-cycle interconnect to on-chip SRAM banks, achieving high per-cycle efficiency for control-heavy workloads. HyperCroc attaches the iDMA data mover to Croc’s main 32-bit Open Bus Interface (OBI) crossbar, giving it single-cycle access to on-chip SRAM. In addition, the iDMA connects directly to the HyperBus controller us-ing OBI with burst support. Contiguous transactions are essential to minimize the overhead of the HyperBus protocol and reach peak sustained bandwidth. Accelerators are attached to the main crossbar through the user domain and can rely on the iDMA + HyperBus path to off-chip memory or on-chip SRAMs for dataset ingress/egress. The HyperBus PHYs run in their own clock domain, up to 200 MHz . Each can be connected to four HyperRAM/HyperFlash devices, providing up to 2 GiB of storage per PHY. At 200 MHz HyperBus reaches the maximum sustained throughput of 400 MB/s for each PHY. With a typical SoC run-ning at 100 MHz or below, this is sufficient to transfer one 32-bit word per cycle. To simplify integration and ease timing closure, the HyperBus PHY is provided as a hardened macro: the high-speed signaling and timing-critical details are con-tained within the macro, leaving a clean, synchronous controller-facing interface for users. For even higher bandwidth scenarios, HyperCroc can instantiate a dual-PHY controller to double external-memory ca-pacity and bandwidth to 2 GiB and 800 MB/s while keeping the rest of the SoC unchanged.

## Conclusion and Outlook 

HyperCroc extends the Croc end-to-end OS RISC-V MCU platform with iDMA and HyperBus, enabling realistic development and benchmarking of domain-specific accelerators, including machine learning in-ference for embedded systems. By strengthening the memory system while retaining a streamlined, repro-ducible OS RTL-to-GDS flow, HyperCroc bridges the gap between educational chip design and the research and prototyping of domain-specific accelerators while building on the same proven foundations. HyperCroc will be taped out March 2026 and released as open source, with silicon validation later this year.
