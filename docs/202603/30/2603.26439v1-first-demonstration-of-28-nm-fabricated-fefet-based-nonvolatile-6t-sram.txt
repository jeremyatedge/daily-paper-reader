# First Demonstration of 28 nm Fabricated FeFET-Based Nonvolatile 6T SRAM 

Albi Mema , Member, IEEE , Simon Thomann , Member, IEEE , Narendra Singh Dhakad ,and Hussam Amrouch , Member, IEEE 

Abstract —With the staggering increase of edge compute applications like Internet-of-Things (IoT) and artificial intelligence (AI), the demand for fast, energy-efficient on-chip memory is growing. While the fast and mature static random-access memory (SRAM) technology is the standard choice, its volatility requires a constant supply voltage to operate and store data. Especially in edge AI and IoT devices that often idle, the leakage power consumes a significant portion of the constrained power budget. For this, emerging non-volatile memory (NVM) technologies such as Resistive RAM and ferroelectric FET (FeFET) offer zero-standby power consumption but suffer from integration and performance tradeoffs. To harness the benefits of the different technologies, hybrid architectures have been proposed, combining SRAM with NVM devices. This work proposes a hybrid non-volatile SRAM (nvSRAM) architecture based on recently demonstrated PMOS FeFETs (p-FeFETs). By replacing the two PMOS pull-up transistors with p-FeFETs, we achieve non-volatility without additional transistors. The design supports seamless power-down and restore operation, thus eliminating standby leakage. SPICE simulations in a commercial 28 nm technology show read latency comparable to conventional SRAM, and on-silicon measurements show robust restore behavior. With this, we are the first to demonstrate a fabricated 6T nvSRAM cell design. The resulting cell achieves an area footprint of 0.99 μm2.The read path remains identical to baseline SRAM, enabling high-speed operation while being non-volatile, making it ideal for IoT and edge systems. 

Index Terms —nvSRAM, Ferroelectric, FeFET, Non-volatile, Ultra-low-power 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

The proliferation of edge computing, Internet-of-Things (IoT), and artificial intelligence (AI) has intensified the demand for fast, energy-efficient on-chip memory. Static random-access memory (SRAM) is the standard choice for these applications due to its high speed, reliability, and technological maturity. In modern AI accelerators SRAM area constitutes up to 80 % 

of the chip area [1]–[4]. However, SRAM is volatile by design and requires a continuous power supply to retain data. In edge AI and IoT devices that remain idle for a significant period and operate in a power-constrained environment, this results in substantial leakage power consumption [5], [6].

To mitigate these drawbacks, emerging non-volatile memory (NVM) technologies such as Resistive RAM (ReRAM) [7]–[11] and Ferroelectric FETs (FeFETs) [12]–[17] have been explored. While these technologies offer zero power consumption to retain data, they often suffer from reliability concerns, high error rates, relatively long write and read latencies, and, in the case of ReRAM, considerably higher write power consumption [18]. Thus, this limits their adoption into AI-centric applications. Consequently, hybrid memory architectures have recently been gaining traction. These architectures leverage both the strengths of SRAM and non-volatile memory approaches. ReRAM [7]–[10], FeFETs [18]–[20], ferroelectric capacitors (FeCAPs) [21], [22] and magnetic tunnel junctions (MTJ) [23]– [25] are some of the non-volatile devices integrated within the SRAM cell. Among these, FeFETs and FeCAPs are particularly promising due to their CMOS compatibility and scalability [26]. However, existing FeFET-based designs face significant trade-offs. For instance, 6T2C architectures [21] are highly sensitive to transistor mismatch, while 6T4C designs [22] incur a 46 % 

area overhead. Other recent non-volatile SRAMs (nvSRAMs) utilize NMOS FeFETs (n-FeFETs) as auxiliary transistors [18]– [20], which increases the silicon footprint and complexity of the memory array. To address these limitations, we propose a novel hybrid nvSRAM architecture, utilizing PMOS FeFETs (p-FeFETs) into a 6T SRAM architecture shown in Fig. 1(a). By replacing the standard PMOS pull-up transistors with p-FeFETs, we achieve non-volatility without adding extra transistors or capacitors. This design enables high-speed operations and seamless power-down states with minimal area overhead, making it ideal for energy-constrained IoT and edge systems. This approach is only possible with the recent fabrication of p-FeFETs. During read operations, the bitlines are precharged to VDD , and the discharge path is dominated by the pull-down NMOS transistor to ground. As a result, the load p-FeFETs do not participate in the read current path, preserving the read performance of the SRAM. Due to its non-volatile nature, the proposed design can be effectively powered down during long idle states without losing the stored information in the bitcells, thereby reducing power consumption from the memory array to zero. B. State-of-the-art nvSRAM Design 

To overcome the challenge of 6T SRAM described above, integrating non-volatile memory elements (ReRAM, MTJ, FeCAP, and FeFET) into SRAM is a common approach to en-able non-volatility while preserving SRAM-level performance. This work focuses on FeFET-based nvSRAM due to their CMOS compatibility, faster switching speeds, lower write energy [20], and higher ION /IOFF ratio. Additionally, FeFETs offer better energy efficiency compared to MTJ- and ReRAM-based designs [49]. State-of-the-art FeFET-based nvSRAMs [18], [20] shown in Fig. 1(c) and 1(d), achieve non-volatility by adding FeFET-based backup and restore circuitry at the storage nodes Q /QB .Li et al. [18] proposed an nvSRAM as shown in Fig. 1(d). While effective at achieving non-volatility, their proposed design requires four additional transistors. This, in turn, introduces higher parasitic capacitances on the internal storage nodes, which degrade both read and write access times. The alternative design from Wang et al. [19] reduces the additional transistor overhead to 2, but it requires independent power-supply control for the cross-coupled inverters and an extra control signal, increasing peripheral complexity. You et al. in [20], shown in Fig. 1(c), proposed a simplified architecture by inserting two FeFETs between Q / QB and the access transistors. Although this approach also incurs minimal transistor-count overhead, it relies on the additional signal line (SL) and gate-write scheme, which adds to the complexity and area overhead. C. FeFET as an Emerging Non-Volatile Memory 

Structurally, the concept of a FeFET is based on a regular MOSFET. By adding a layer of ferroelectric (FE) material to the gate stack, the transistor characteristic can be shifted through the polarization state of the FE layer. The polarization of the FE layer in a FeFET is changed by applying a strong voltage bias, e.g., ±4 V , to its gate (see Fig. 2(a) and 2(b)). This VOL. XX, NO. XX, XXXX 2026 4

flips the domains inside the FE layer to align with the electric field established by the gate potential. The polarization interacts with the perceived gate voltage in the channel, amplifying or dampening it depending on its direction. This shifts the transfer characteristic of the FeFET, which is usually described as a shift in threshold voltage ( VTH ) and ultimately changes the resistance at a given gate voltage. Using the two distinct polarization states allows for storing information in the FE layer, labeled LVT and HVT for the saturated positive/negative directions, respectively. The opening between the LVT and HVT states in the ID-VG

characteristic is called memory window (MW) and used as a metric for the separation of the two states. Additionally, FeFET provides non-volatile information storage, as the polarization state persists even when power is cut, making FeFET an NVM. With the discovery of doped HfO 2-based FE materials, a thin layer (e.g., 10 nm ) is sufficient, enabling further scalability of FeFET [50]. Following the introduction of doped HfO 2-based FeFETs, GlobalFoundries demonstrated the fabrication of 22 nm Fully-Depleted Silicon on Insulator (FDSOI) FeFETs, emphasizing the scalability [31]. The recent demonstration of PMOS-based FeFETs enables new design opportunities for circuits using FeFET technology [51]. The multi-domain structure of the FE layer allows for a partial polarization of the FeFET. In addition to the classical gate-driven polarization scheme as shown in Fig. 2(a) and 2(b), an electric field can also be established by biasing the source and/or drain terminal [52], [53]. Essential for the polarization process is a strong enough potential drop across the FE layer that forces the domains to align their polarization direction. How this potential difference is established is up to the designer and the circuit’s constraints. Fig. 2(c) shows the biasing using the source and drain terminal with +4 V and a grounded gate. This achieves the same direction and electric field strength as the gate biasing with −4 V (see Fig. 2(b)), thus polarizing the transistor to LVT, and can be considered equivalent. Using only the drain (or the source) terminal to establish the electric field, as shown in Fig. 2(d), will polarize the FE portion close to the drain terminal the strongest and weaken towards the source terminal. This will result in an overall weaker HVT polarization state as shown in Fig. 3 [52]. III. D ESIGN OF OUR PROPOSED NV SRAM C ELL 

To overcome the complexity and performance limitations of existing non-volatile memory architectures, we propose a hybrid nvSRAM architecture that preserves the baseline 6T SRAM topology. Our design, as shown in Fig. 1(a), integrates p-FeFETs as the load transistors (M2 and M4). The proposed design preserves the peripheral architecture of the baseline 6T SRAM while enabling non-volatile operation. Read and hold modes are identical to the baseline design, ensuring that the critical read path, sense circuitry, and timing remain unchanged. Non-volatility is achieved by programming the load p-FeFETs, which require elevated supply voltage. This elevated supply voltage can be implemented using standard multiplexing without the need for negative voltages and non-standard drivers. Because the read current path is unchanged and the load devices do not directly participate in the bitline’s discharge during read, the proposed design maintains conventional read performance while avoiding complex peripheral circuitry. This compatibility allows the proposed nvSRAM to integrate seamlessly into existing SRAM macro design flows. 

A. Write Operation 

The simplicity of the peripheral interface arises from the fact that the logical write sequence is identical to the baseline 6T SRAM, with only the amplitude temporarily increased. The bitlines (BL and BLB) are driven to complementary levels while the WL is asserted to propagate these voltages to the internal nodes Q and QB . These nodes are connected to the drain and gate of the load p-FeFETs M2 and M4. However, to ensure non-volatile storage, the write voltage is increased to 4 V and applied with a longer pulse duration to reliably program the ferroelectric layer. The programming schemes illustrated in Fig. 2, show that gate-drain and gate-source driven schemes produce asymmetric polarization strengths. This asymmetry is quantified in Fig. 3, where the achievable memory window using these two schemes is smaller compared to conventional schemes. With this scheme, the p-FeFET cannot be fully polarized to the HVT state. However, this is not problematic because during nominal operation, the gate-source potential that the transistor in HVT and LVT will see are 

0 V and −1 V respectively, assuming maximum 1 V power supply. The transistor in the HVT state is completely off at this potential while the transistor in LVT is strongly conducting. During this phase, the power supply is kept at 4 V . This prevents unintended conduction during the drain-write program-ming. The load p-FeFET with 0 V at its gate is conductive; if there is a potential difference between its source and drain, then current can flow easily from the supply to the bitline, effectively creating a short. Increasing the power supply to 

4 V avoids this issue. This bias condition corresponds to the source and drain programming scheme in Fig. 2(c), where the ferroelectric layer polarizes strongly to the LVT state. If the incoming data matches the existing state, no polar-ization switching occurs (Fig. 4). If the data is different, the p-FeFET experiencing VG = 4 V , VS = 4 V and VD = 0 V ,switches from the LVT state to an HVT state. Simultaneously, the other p-FeFET shifts towards the LVT state due to the potential difference across its gate, source and drain; VG = 0 V ,

VS = 4 V , and VD = 4 V . Fig. 4 visualizes the state-dependent 

VTH evolution of M2 and M4 during write operation. When writing the opposite state, the polarization of the two load p-FeFETs swaps, producing complementary VTH shifts. The voltage on different nodes of the nvSRAM cell for the case of logic 1 write are shown in Fig. 5(a). After the polarization pulse is applied to BL and BLB, the WL is de-asserted. The voltages at nodes Q and QB return to their nominal values of 

1 V . Note that during this hold state, the voltage values on Qand QB , despite being smaller than the voltages required for polarization, reinforce the polarization of the FeFET transistors. This guarantees stable data and prevents random bit flips.

During the restore or power-on operation, the power supply is ramped up to the operation voltage of 1 V . The ramp-up of VDD cannot be arbitrarily fast. The restore process relies on the current imbalance between the HVT and LVT polarized p-FeFETs, which must develop before the cross-coupled inverter pair enters strong positive feedback. As shown in Fig. 3, a current imbalance exists across the gate-source voltage range from −1 V to 0 V since the maximum supply voltage VDD is 

1 V . However, the amplitude of this current differential depends on the voltage potential on the storage nodes Q and QB before the restore. Due to process variation and noise from the power-up ramp, the value of the current imbalance might be very small, which might force the circuit to latch into an unstable state and in the worst case scenario a short circuit state. The soft-start via the slow power-up help prevent this problem by limiting noise and allowing the positive feedback to place the storage nodes to the expected values incrementally. In addition to process variation, residual charge can accu-mulate on nodes Q and QB due to the surrounding fields by interconnects in the proximity of the cell, potentially placing them outside the expected −1 V to 0 V range. This and process variation might lead to a sign change in the differential current between the two load p-FeFETs. This may lead to incorrect data latching during restore. In this case, the restore sequence can be extended to a two-phase approach where first the BL and BLB are pulled to 0 V with the WL asserted. This ensures that both load p-FeFETs see the same potential on their gate, drain and source during power-up. The transistor polarized to the LVT state is able to drive more current in the next phase of VDD ramp-up to which its drain is connected to. Because one p-FeFET conducts more current, one of the nodes Q and QB is pulled faster towards the supply voltage. At a voltage level determined by the polarization state, the positive feedback cross-coupled inverter takes over. As shown in Fig. 5(c), for the case when 1 is stored, due to the positive feedback M1 pulls down the node QB to ground while the load p-FeFET M4 pushes charges to the node Q . The nvSRAM cell then latches to the correct data state. 

C. Read Operation 

The read operation of the proposed design is identical to that of the baseline 6T SRAM. Both bitlines are precharged and WL is asserted. The stored data causes a differential discharge of BL and BLB, which is detected by a sense amplifier. Fig. 5(d) shows the biasing of the nvSRAM cell during a logic 1 read operation. Read operations are performed at VDD = 1 V . Since the read current path flows through the pull-down network, the load p-FeFETs do not participate in the discharge path. This preserves conventional read performance. 

D. Hold Operation 

The hold operation functions identically to the baseline 6T SRAM with the data actively retained through the cross-coupled inverters. However, the primary advantage of the proposed nvSRAM is the ability to power down the array during idle periods, reducing standby energy consumption to effectively zero. IV. S IMULATION RESULTS OF

C. FeFET Reliability Discussion 

Endurance: The endurance of FeFETs is a concern in this use case as SRAM itself offers a high endurance of >10 15 .Endurance measurements of 28 nm FeFET devices shows an endurance of 10 5 for n-FeFET and 10 4 for p-FeFET [56]. This means that the non-volatile storage of data in the p-FeFETs can not be used as frequently as changing the state of the underlying SRAM cell. If needed, the endurance can be increased by utilizing lower programming voltages (e.g., 3.3 V 

instead of 4.5 V ) [57]. 

Disturbance: During the hold of the nvSRAM cell, one of the p-FeFET transistors has a constant gate bias applied. The polarization process also depends on the pulse width, i.e., the duration of the applied pulse. Hence, a weaker electric field applied for a long duration may also switch the domain’s polarization, raising concerns about potential disturbance. However, the halid plot based on p-FeFET 28 nm 

measurements shown in Fig. 11 links the switching dynamics of pulse amplitude and width. Stronger amplitudes need shorter pulses to shift the polarization from, e.g., HVT to 50 % LVT. The data shows, for voltages below 2 V , long pulses ( >100 s )are needed to change the polarization, if it is changing at all. Therefore, with a VDD of 1 V , the electric field from the gate and drain node is small enough not to cause disturbance in the ferroelectric state. Additionally, as stated earlier, the bias of Q and QB during the hold operation positively reinforces the polarization. 

Retention: To serve as a NVM, the retention is an important metric. It estimates the time over which the polarization state is retained. In our use case, this means for how long the nvSRAM cell can remain powered off, hold the state in the p-FeFETs, and latch to the correct state when restored. For the used 28 nm 

process, the retention is extrapolated to be 10 years at a stress temperature of 105 ◦C [26]. With it, our proposed nvSRAM cell design can retain the state over its entire estimated lifetime. VI. C ONCLUSION 

Utilizing the recently demonstrated p-FeFET we have designed a novel nvSRAM cell without the need for additional transistors. By replacing the pull-up PMOS transistors with p-FeFETs, we achieve a non-volatile cell that offers a reliable power-down and restore. Using simulation, we tested the proposed concept and analyzed its reliability using SNM. For silicon verification, the design was taped-out using a 28 nm 

process and measured. The measurements confirm the proof of concept and successful power-up using different supply voltages. From the layout of our proposed nvSRAM cell, the area footprint consumed is 0.99 μm2.ACKNOWLEDGMENT
