# TensorPool: A 3D-Stacked 8.4TFLOPS/4.3W Many-Core Domain-Specific Processor for AI-Native Radio Access Networks 

Marco Bertuletti, Yichao Zhang, Diyou Shen, Alessandro Vanelli-Coralli, Frank K. G¨ urkaynak, Luca Benini, 

Abstract —The upcoming integration of AI in the physical layer (PHY) of 6G radio access networks (RAN) will enable a higher quality of service in challenging transmission scenarios. However, deeply optimized AI-Native PHY models impose higher computational complexity compared to conventional baseband, challenging deployment under the sub-msec real-time constraints typical of modern PHYs. Additionally, following the extension to terahertz carriers, the upcoming densification of 6G cell-sites fur-ther limits the power consumption of base stations, constraining the budget available for compute ( ≤ 100 W). The desired flexibil-ity to ensure long term sustainability and the imperative energy-efficiency gains on the high-throughput tensor computations dom-inating AI-Native PHYs can be achieved by domain-specialization of many-core programmable baseband processors. Following the domain-specialization strategy, we present TensorPool, a cluster of 256 RISCV32IMAF programmable cores, accelerated by 16 256 MACs/cycle (FP16) tensor engines with low-latency access to 4MiB of L1 scratchpad for maximal data-reuse. Implemented in TSMC’s N7, TensorPool achieves 3643 MACs/cycle (89% tensor-unit utilization) on tensor operations for AI-RAN, 6 × more than a core-only cluster without tensor acceleration, while simultane-ously improving GOPS/W/mm 2 efficiency by 9.1 ×. Further, we show that 3D-stacking the computing blocks of TensorPool to better unfold the tensor engines to L1-memory routing provides 2.32 × footprint improvement with no frequency degradation, compared to a 2D implementation. 

Index Terms —6G, AI, RAN, many-core, RISC-V 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

The IMT-2030 recommendation for the next-generation Radio Access Networks (RANs) [1] promotes the dense in-tegration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and communication, with two main advantages. First, a Machine Learning (ML) based RAN is expected to support self-monitoring, self-organization, self-optimization, and self-healing under various traffic conditions. Second, empowering the RAN infrastructure with hardware for ML accelerated computing will create a distributed computer for the AI computing-continuum [2]. This will ensure seamless user experiences across devices from the edge to the cloud and enable distributed intelligent and data-driven applications. Focusing on the first objective, on-field studies demonstrated that AI has an edge over model-driven approaches [3]: it can learn scenarios where explicit modeling is infeasible, it can make real-time decisions in high-dimensional design-spaces, and it can perform accurate prediction and proactive optimization. Layer (PHY), where ML based solutions were applied to channel estimation, beamforming, dynamic power adjustment, and interference mitigation, but also in the data link layer and the network layer, where AI solutions were devised for adaptive modulation and coding selection, dynamic resource allocation and load balancing, multi-cell scheduling and mo-bility management optimizations [4], [5]. However, as 6G RAN scales with bandwidth, antenna elements, and number of cells, the compute demands of AI-for-RAN workloads grow commensurately [6]. In particular, the processing of AI-Native PHY is subject to tight latency and power consumption constraints. PHY tasks typically must be completed within 1 ms. To meet this strict time bound, PHY processing must be executed on the edge, in the base stations. Additionally, since the evolution to 6G forecasts an increase in cell-site density, base station power consumption must be kept low, allowing only up to 100W power budget for digital signal processing [7], [8]. In this regard, Central Processing Units (CPUs), Field Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGAs), and Application Specific Integrated Circuits (ASICs) span a wide flexibility versus efficiency tradeoff range, but none alone can unite rapid pro-grammability, elastic compute scaling, low-latency and low-power execution, and adaptability to new workloads or chang-ing requirements required by AI-Native RAN [6]. A new class of programmable AI-specialized baseband edge processors is needed, to support high-throughput PHY workloads within a power envelope of tens of watts, while at the same time providing sufficient flexibility for real-time reconfiguration and long term sustainability. The currently available many-core compute platforms for PHY processing in Table I have the potential to fill this gap: they offer hundreds of general-purpose programmable processors to process in parallel software-defined AI-for-RAN workloads, non-AI RAN workloads, and edge AI services on one device. However, these platforms also present limitations. Datacenter Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) show significant power consumption [12], making them unsuitable for edge deployments. Smaller many-core RAN processors [9]–[11]

achieve lower performance than datacenter GPUs, but meet the power consumption requirements of base stations. Among them, the TeraPool many-core cluster [9] has the lowest power consumption and the highest number of Processing Elements (PEs) with shared low-latency access to L1 memory. This fea-ture allows parallelizing large-dimensional PHY workloads on hundreds of PEs, without fragmenting them across many small clusters, thereby preventing expensive data movement, as well as distribution and synchronization overheads across the memory hierarchy [8]. Nevertheless, the key problem to tackle for AI-native RAN feasibility, is scaling the performance of such many-core baseband processors to ML processing needs, at controlled power consumption and high energy efficiency. From a technology-centric viewpoint, 3D stacking of mem-ory and logic can help meeting the high performance and efficiency requirements of AI-Native RAN edge workloads. In the high-performance computing market, 2.5D integration with High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) is a common solution to feed data-hungry Neural Processing Units (NPUs) [13]– [15]. However, this approach is expensive and power-hungry (due to the high cost and power consumption of HBM memory stacks), and it does not apply to dense edge deployments, such as those envisioned for AI-RAN. For this reason, 3D stacked chiplet architectures [16], where multiple memory and/or compute layers are vertically integrated to increase memory capacity and compute density, are expanding from servers toward embedded and edge computing platforms [17]. In this context, we outline our main contributions: 1) We analyze AI-PHY models according to computational complexity and memory footprint. From this analysis, we identify a significant performance gap for many-core PHY processors when targeting AI workloads, and propose domain-specific architectural specialization focused on General Matrix-Matrix Multiply (GEMM)-dominated computation. 2) We devise TensorPool, an AI-native RAN processor. We integrate 16 Tensor Engines (TEs) and 256 PEs with 4 MiB of shared L1 memory through a hierarchical inter-connect, and increase 16-bit Floating-Point (FP16) peak-performance by 2.25 × (8.4 TFLOPS@FP16), compared to a homogeneous cluster of RISC-V PEs. 3) We design a novel microarchitecture for the TEs’ mem-ory interface to tolerate the latency of the cluster’s physically distributed memory hierarchy and enable multiple TEs to operate in parallel at high utilization on large GEMM workloads. In particular, we integrate burst support into the L1 large cluster interconnect to better serve the increased burst bandwidth injected by TEs. We demonstrate analytically and with RTL-simulation experiments that the proposed architecture is not bound by the L1-memory bandwidth. With a streamlined paral-lelization scheme, we achieve up to 89% parallel FP16-Multiply&Add unit (FMA) utilization on GEMM and up to 67% utilization on domain-specific tasks, including data-transfers overheads and concurrent PE operation. 4) We demonstrate that a placed&routed instance of Ten-sorPool in TSMC’s 7nm FinFET (N7) node achieves 57.53 GFLOPS@FP16/W/mm 2 on tensor operations (9.1 × higher than the homogeneous cluster baseline, thanks to the increase in compute-units number and utilization). 5) We show that with 3D-integration and routing in the ver-tical direction, the area of the routing channels required to connect multiple high-bandwidth TEs to memory banks can be reduced by 67 %, leading to superlinear (2.32 ×) footprint decrease and opening a promising new avenue for scaling performance and efficiency. This work demonstrates for the first time that it is possible to meet the throughput and power consumption requirements of upcoming AI-Native RAN PHYs with a flexible, heteroge-neous shared L1 large-scale cluster with tensor acceleration. Furthermore, we show for the first time that logic-on-logic 3D integration provides additional headroom for scaling up the performance and efficiency of shared L1 many-cores. M ODELS FOR AI-N ATIVE RAN In this section, we classify existing Neural Network (NN)-based models for AI-Native RAN, to specify the required peak performance and motivate the target workload. Figure 1 reports AI-Native PHY models [18]–[27], classi-fied according to their architecture, memory footprint (in terms of trainable parameters), number of operations, and target task. All models demonstrated better quality-of-service metrics compared to classical signal processing algorithms. 3

Based on their target task, the models can be divided into two groups: models that implement the entire Orthogonal Fre-quency Division Multiple Access (OFDMA) uplink receiver chain [18]–[24], and models that implement a single uplink function [25]–[27]. In particular, we focus on models for Channel Estimation (CHE): the estimation of the transmission channel represents a problem difficult to describe under clas-sical assumptions, especially for large-dimensional channels with moving users, which is a key use-case for AI in PHY tackled by [26], [27]. CHE models typically do not need to be used on the full transmission bandwidth but can be dynamically assigned to users requiring a better quality of service in the current transmission slot. For this reason, they were trained on a lower number of Physical Resource Blocks (PRBs), compared to full OFDMA receivers. Nevertheless, we observe that normalizing by the number of PRBs the number of operations required by CHE models, we obtain a computational complexity compa-rable to the least expensive full OFDMA receivers [21], [22]. This suggests that a model encompassing the entire uplink can achieve lower computational complexity and memory footprint compared to models trained on a focus task. A flexible processor for AI-Native wireless processing should support both approaches. Therefore, we target a computing platform that can jointly perform classical wireless signal processing and NN workloads. Models on the upper end of computational complexity [18]– [20], [23], [24] were designed to run on centralized servers or in the cloud [28], [29]. We decide instead to tune the peak performance of our architecture to the state-of-the-art for real-time edge deployment [22]. Taking 1 ms Transmission Time Interval (TTI) latency as a real-time reference constraint, and considering the most demanding use-case of [22], we conclude that a many-core for AI-RAN processing should support at least 6 TFLOPS of AI computations, 1.67 × larger than what is offered by the state-of-the-art TeraPool processor. In terms of memory footprint, aiming for minimizing data movement across the memory hierarchy, the many-core’s L1-memory should contain the TTI input samples and the model parameters. In this regard, excluding the models for cloud deployment, and assuming FP16 arithmetic precisions, all other approaches fit in 4 MiB. Finally, we observe that the proposed survey contains both ResNet-inspired convolutional models [18]–[22] and attention-based models [23], [24], both heavily dominated by GEMM computations. Therefore, we can obtain the required improve-ments in computational complexity, while keeping low power consumption, by domain-specialization of our architecture focused on GEMM operations. III. A RCHITECTURE 

Based on the analysis of state-of-the-art models for AI-Native PHY, we design TensorPool, a domain-specific many-core heterogeneous architecture combining general-purpose PEs and TEs. TensorPool architecture achieves 

> 8 TFLOPS@FP16 peak performance, by integrating domain-specific GEMM accelerators with 4 MiB of shared multi-banked L1 scratchpad. 

A. TensorPool’s Interconnect 

The Pool is a modular design, assembled bottom up from 

Tiles . A Tile in Figure 2-a, contains 4 PEs, each capable of two FP16-MACs/cycle in parallel on their 32-bit Floating-Point Unit (FPU), a shared Div-Sqrt FPU, 32 ×2KiB memory banks, and 4 KiB shared L1-I$. Scheme for response grouping under the same handshake. FP16-MACs/cycle combined throughput, 2.25 × larger than in TeraPool’s 64-PEs SubGroup. In the TE, FMAs are arranged in R = 32 rows by C = 8 

columns. Three data buffers (Y&Z share the same) fed by a streamer provide data to the compute elements. The streamer loads and stores data through a C ×(P +1) ×16-bit = 512-bit wide memory port. Each TE-row computes a dot-product between an X-row and a W-column, accumulating in a Y-element. On each TE column, the same X value is kept station-ary and is multiplied by different columns of W. The FMAs have P = 3 pipeline stages. At each cycle they pass over the partial dot-product to the next pipeline stage. To keep FMAs always busy, the TE must load from W C × (P + 1) elements every four cycles, while it refills the X and Y buffers. The TE’s controller exposes a set of configuration registers, programmed in software by one of the Tile’s PEs. After programming the TE, the PE is free to execute independent code upon receiving an interrupt request by the TE on workload completion. A key challenge in TensorPool is to connect 16 TEs to all the distributed scratchpad memory banks through the hierarchical multi-cycle interconnect, while hiding memory latency and achieving full FMA-utilization. In [30] and [31], the latency of tightly coupled memories is hidden by data buffers: a streamer is allowed continuous pre-fetching of data until the buffer reaches its capacity. However, the streamer assumes in-order responses, which is unfortunately not what happens in a system with non-uniform memory latencies. To overcome this mismatch, which would lead to significant TE stalls and underutilization, we designed the latency-tolerant streamer in Figure 3. It includes a 16-entry Reorder Buffer (ROB) on each stream, X, W, Y. The ROB keeps track of in-flight reads and allows to pipeline multiple outstanding transactions in the multi-cycle interconnect. A transactions table is also necessary to collect 32-bit bank responses, and commit 512-bit wide responses to the TE. Finally, a 32-entry FIFO on the Z stream allows us to empty the Y/Z buffers upon dot-product completion and immediately pre-load new Ys. The Tile’s arbiter can only retire up to 7 transactions per cycle, four addressing the Tiles of SubGroups within a Group and 3 addressing Tiles in remote Groups. This represents a bottleneck for the wide TEs requests leaving the Tile, which get narrowed to the arbiter’s bandwidth and are dispatched in multiple cycles. This creates backpressure on the streamer and blocks new requests. To avoid serialization of wide requests at the Tile’s arbiter, we take advantage of the burst-based access patterns of TEs to memory, and augment the interconnect with lightweight burst support [32]. As shown in Figure 4, a Burst-Grouper 

in the initiator’s Tile intercepts a 512-bit wide read-request, transforming it into a burst : only the address to the first 32-bit element in the 512-bit request is sent to the arbiter and crosses the hierarchical interconnects. Subsequent wide requests are let through the arbiter in the next cycle, restoring a peak throughput of 7 wide read transactions per cycle. A Burst-Distributor in the destination Tile identifies burst-requests and generates 16 narrow requests to target banks. Serialization of responses at the local crossbar of the des-tination Tile can also be partially avoided by increasing the interconnect bandwidth. We achieve that while avoiding the introduction of additional ports in the crossbars by widening the data field only of our custom protocol. In fact, we group K

responses under the same valid/ready handshake, limiting the impact of additional read bandwidth on the routing complexity. Figure 3-b is an example of the adopted strategy for K = 2 . A similar approach is also adopted for wide write-requests: the width of the request data field is multiplied by a factor J = 2 .

C. L2 Memory Interconnect 

The system is completed by a top-level AXI-XBAR, ac-cessed in parallel by all Tiles through the hierarchical AXI-interconnect described in [9], and delivering up to 512-bit/cycle read and write bandwidth to each SubGroup. The XBAR connects TensorPool to L2 and allows any PE to program a centralized Direct Memory Access (DMA) engine through a register-based interface. The DMA is responsible for sending data requests to L2 and redistributing responses through the hierarchical AXI-interconnect to the memory banks in all Tiles. IV. M EMORY BALANCES 

In this section, we demonstrate that thanks to the high-bandwidth L2 memory connection, the TEs are not bound by L2 memory transfers. Moreover, we demonstrate that thanks to the multiple-outstanding burst-transaction feature and the enlarged response bandwidth of shared L1 memory interconnects, the single-TE is not bound by L1 memory latency. These theoretical results are backed by experimental validation, where we demonstrate near-ideal utilization of the single-TE’s FMAs.

C. Performance of Models for AI-Native PHY Executed in Parallel on TEs&PEs 

We benchmark the performance of TensorPool in the case of concurrent operation of its computation and data-movement engines: TEs, PEs and DMA. In particular, we focus on the three use-cases represented in Figure 9: a Fully-Connected (FC) layer with GEMM and softmax activation, a depthwise-separable convolution with layer normalization and ReLU, a Multi-Head Attention (MHA). These compute blocks are at the core of the models described in Section II. For each block, we propose a sequential implementation, where we operate TEs, PEs and DMA one at a time, and a concurrent implementation, where computation and data transfer can overlap in time. The temporal diagram of the computations and transfers divided on TEs, PEs, and DMA is in Figure 10. The FC layer with row-wise softmax activation is used in all models in our survey. For the concurrent operation of PEs, TEs, and DMA, we propose a double-buffer implementation: while TEs compute GEMM on the data of the current iteration, PEs execute softmax on the result of the previous GEMM iteration. The DMA transfers data from L2 to L1 for the next iteration, and the softmax output to L2 after TEs and PEs complete the processing. The runtime results in Figure 10 correspond to a 512 ×512 input matrix. The depthwise-separable convolution with layer normaliza-tion and ReLU is at the core of the ResNet-based models for OFDMA [18]–[24]. The depthwise-separable convolution can be split in a depthwise 2D-convolution, followed by a pointwise 1D-convolution. The 1D-convolution can be easily mapped to a matrix-matrix multiplication running on TEs, with accumulation along the input tensor depth. The depthwise 2D-convolution can be executed in parallel on PEs. We consider a double-buffer implementation, where these two operations are executed concurrently on PEs and TEs for two different iterations. The runtime results in Figure 10 correspond to 3 ×32D-convolutional filters applied to 32 ×16 input frames with 512-element depth. The MHA block is present in multiple CHE models [23]– [25]. A. 3D Partitioning 

The four Groups of TensorPool can be split across two dies: the top die contains two of the Groups and routing, the bottom die contains the other two Groups, system registers, the register-based frontend to program DMA transfers and the top-level AXI-XBAR. In Figure 14 we report a schematic floorplan with explicit connection between Groups for a single 2D chip and for the desired stacked 3D wafer-to-wafer hybrid-bonded TensorPool. The 3D implementation achieves two main im-provements. First, it eliminates one of the routing channels marked with red-shading on the 2D floorplan, reducing the routing area. Second, it also eliminates diagonal connections between groups in the limited area at the center of the die. Connections to diagonally opposed Groups can now be spread in the large channel between Groups and routed horizontally in one die, then vertically, crossing the dies’ boundary, and finally horizontally again in the other die, reducing crossings and routing congestion. The expected reduction in routing channels area can be computed analytically based on metal stack, bond pitch and geometrical floorplanning information. B. 3D Performance and Area 

The 3D floorplan for the Group instances is represented in Figure 16. We envision a stack of two silicon dies with thermal dissipation over the top-die, power delivery from the back side of the bottom-die and power grid on Mz metal layers in the bottom-die BEOL [16], [36]. We demonstrate that the connections between Groups can be routed in the 3D stacked chip without a significant impact on timing closure caused by hybrid-bonding. To do so, we implement the Groups as black-box instances, applying timing constraints compatible with the post placement and routing results obtained in the 2D design. We assume a fine face-to-face bond pitch of 4.5 μm, with 1 μm

× 1μm size, 0.5 Ω resistance, and 1 fF capactance [38]. We define the 3D placement of bottom-die, top-die and bonds as in Figure 16 using Synopsys 3DIC Compiler 2025.06. We then complete the placement and routing of the separate die instances in Synopsys Fusion Compiler 2025.06. Finally, we verify the full-stack timing closure in Synopsys 3DIC Compiler. Each 3D stacked die occupies an 11.47 mm 2 area. Com-pared to the 2D floorplan, the area of the routing channels is reduced by 67%, from 5.59 mm 2 in 2D to 0.91 mm 2 on each 3D stacked die. The total wire length of the 3D global routes between the Pool Groups is 20 % shorter than in the 2D implementation, while the two designs exhibit a similar level of routing congestion. This result indicates that exploiting the Z direction brings Group macros closer and reduces the wiring distance. For this reason, the 3D design achieves a 2.32 × smaller footprint compared to its 2D counterpart, which is higher than the expected linear 2 × improvement for a 3D design stacked on 2-tiers. Concerning timing, the longest cross-tier path corresponds to a remote L1-memory access between Groups located at opposite corners of the two-tier stack. Including the delay of driving buffers and bonding terminals, this route consists of around 120 ps at the typical corner (TT, 0.75 V , 25 ◦C), which corresponds to only 10 % of the clock period. The SubGroup limits the operating frequency of the design. In summary, our analysis shows that 3D-integration offers significant benefits and opens a clear opportunity for scaling the domain-specific TensorPool cluster to match the rapidly evolving memory and compute requirements of future AI-Native RANs. VIII. S OA C OMPARISON 

The academic literature provides several examples of high throughput and high energy efficiency ASIC implementations of classical wireless signal processing algorithms [39]–[44]. Implementing a fixed function, these circuits cannot be re-purposed to the emerging AI-Native RAN workloads. State-of-the-art Application Specific Instruction Processor (ASIP) solutions for baseband [45]–[50] also have limited recon-figurability, because they implement a small instruction set overspecialized for traditional 5G PHY processing. While academia lacks concrete examples of programmable baseband processors with the flexibility and performance re-quired to migrate to AI-Native RAN, in Table III, we compare TensorPool and TensorPool-3D with commercial GPU based platforms and tensor accelerators for AI-native RAN. The Qualcomm HTA platform [51] provides attractive per-formance per MHz and number of TEs, but offers limited compute capability (2 TOPS), positioning it for lightweight inference tasks rather than full-scale AI-RAN processing. The Aerial RAN systems [52]–[57], achieve the highest tensor throughput, reaching up to 503.8 TOPS, by scaling-out the Streaming Multiprocessor (SM), a modular shared-L1 compute cluster with 128 KiB of L1-memory, 128 PEs and 4 TEs. This high performance comes at the cost of substantial power consumption (up to 600 W, significantly higher than TensorPool’s). Consequently, these platforms are well-suited for centralized deployments [28], but are not suited for energy-constrained base-station deployment scenarios. The performance of TensorPool and TensorPool-3D is tai-lored to the maximum compute capability required by AI-Native RAN models. Compared to a SM, where the L1 memory is shared by 4 TEs only, the 16 TEs of a TensorPool cluster deliver 4.76 × higher performance. Additionally, our Pools have 32 × larger L1 memory than a SM, which allows us to store larger problem sizes and greatly reduce data transfers to higher memory hierarchies. TensorPool-3D achieves a com-parable area efficiency to the Aerial designs (288 GOPS/mm 2), once taking into account the improvement offered by tech-nology scaling. Moreover, it demonstrates 1.16 × higher area-efficiency than its 2D counterpart and a superlinear 2.32 ×

footprint reduction, highlighting the benefits of a compact 3D-design and opening further scale-up opportunities. IX. C ONCLUSION 

In this work, we presented TensorPool, a many-core cluster with 256 lightweight PEs accelerated by 16 256-FMAs TEs. TeraPool’s heterogeneous design combines TEs and PEs to target the intensive tensor computations in AI-PHY, at a power consumption compatible with edge basestation deployments. The low-latency interconnect, the novel TEs memory interface and the streamlined parallelization scheme ensure peak 89% parallel FMA-utilization on GEMM: 3 × the FPU-utilization of TeraPool, a shared-memory Pool with PEs only. 11
