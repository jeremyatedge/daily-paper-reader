Abstract —The introduction of dynamic power management strategies such as clock gating and power gating in dataflow networks has been shown to provide significant energy savings when applied during idle times. However, these strategies can also degrade throughput due to shutdown and wake-up delays. Such throughput degradations might be particularly detrimental to signal processing systems that require a guaranteed throughput. As a solution, this paper first contributes a linear-program formulation for finding a periodic maximal-throughput schedule of a given so-called self-powering dataflow network where actors, realized in hardware, are allowed to go to sleep whenever not being enabled to fire. Depending on which actors are allowed to power down, tradeoffs between throughput and energy savings can be obtained. As a second contribution, we propose amixed-integer-linear-program formulation to determine a peri-odic schedule that satisfies a given throughput while minimizing the overall energy per period by identifying a respective set of actors that is allowed to power down in phases of idleness and which rather not. Finally, as a third contribution, we propose a multi-objective design-space exploration strategy called “Hop and Skip” to efficiently explore the Pareto front of energy and throughput solutions. Experimental evaluations on a set of existing benchmarks and randomly generated graphs witness significant exploration time reductions over a brute-force sweep. Finally, a real-world case study is elaborated and we report on achievable energy savings and throughputs of the related dataflow network where (a) all actors are always-active, (b) all actors are self-powered, and (c) all optimal energy and throughput tradeoff points as found by the proposed design-space exploration strategy. 

Index Terms —marked graph, ILP, throughput, self-powering dataflow, energy-efficient, DSE 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Modern signal processing applications, such as image and video analysis, face the challenge of being both computa-tionally demanding and energy-constrained, arising from their realization in Internet of Things (IoT) devices that rely on ambient energy sources or batteries [1], [2]. To model these applications, we can use Dataflow Graphs (DFGs), which con-sist of a network of interconnected actors —the computational kernel nodes—and channels —the communication edges [3], [4]. An actor is enabled to execute once specific conditions, referred to as firing rules , are met. Upon firing, tokens are consumed from the input channels, and resultant tokens are produced on the output channels of an actor. DFGs enable the exploitation of concurrency of multiple actors operating and firing concurrently (global parallelism) as well as within the

implementation of actors (local parallelism). To exploit such parallelism, there exist code-generation frameworks such as [5] that transform applications concisely described in a domain-specific language into hardware implementations that realize each application as a DFG. Recently, there has been a growing focus in research on developing implementations of dataflow applications that are not only throughput-optimized but also energy-efficient [6]. The concept of Self-powering Dataflow Networks [7], [8] exploits the idea to allow actor implementations to power down during idle times, for example, when waiting for input data. However, bringing an actor into sleep mode and waking it up again may introduce not only the problem of conditional execution times, but even non-negligible additional execution-time delays, raising the two issues of (a) how to properly analyze systems that exhibit dynamically arising execution-time delays, and (b) deciding if an actor should shut down at all to meet a given throughput constraint. The paper is structured as follows: Section II introduces the required fundamentals of DFGs, self-powered dataflow, and throughput analysis. The 1st issue is addressed in Section III by proposition of a Linear Program (LP) formulation for analyzing the throughput of self-powering dataflow networks, while the 2nd issue is solved by introducing a Mixed-Integer-Linear-Program (MILP) formulation to determine a periodic schedule that satisfies a given throughput constraint while minimizing the overall energy per period by identifying a respective set of actors that is allowed to power down in phases of idleness and which rather not. Section IV presents our 3rd contribution, a novel Design Space Exploration (DSE) strategy called Hop and Skip (H&S) , to determine tradeoffs between throughput and energy across different DFG implementations. Section V introduces a real-world case study to analyze achievable energy savings and throughputs of DFGs where (a) all actors are always-active, (b) all actors are self-powered, and (c) all optimal energy and throughput tradeoff points, as found by the proposed DSE strategy. Additionally, evaluations on a set of DFG benchmarks and 100 random DFGs demon-strate that the H&S strategy significantly reduces exploration time. Finally, related work is discussed in Section VI, and Section VII concludes the paper. II. F UNDAMENTALS AND BACKGROUND

B. Self-Powering Dataflow Networks 

In [7], the concept of self-powering dataflow networks has been introduced. Motivated by the fact that in recent circuit technologies, even the idle power of actors can be quite substantial, the authors of [8] proposed to fully exploit modern power-saving techniques like clock-gating and power-gating to reduce the power and thereby energy consumption of circuits implementing DFGs further. A mode where, during times of non-fireability, an actor is put into a low-power (sleep) mode using techniques such as clock-gating or power-gating will be referred to as the Self-Powered (SP) mode of operation in the following.

However, if an actor operates in self-powered mode, a throughput penalty may result due to the induced wake-up delay. Our techniques presented in the following shall analyze throughput and energy-saving tradeoffs by choosing whether to implement an actor to execute in always-active or in self-powered mode, calling such implementations hybrid . But first, we want to reason about the existence of periodic schedules for such hybrid implementations at all, as execution delays obviously depend on whether an actor is executed in always-active or in self-powered mode. III. M AXIMAL THROUGHPUT AND MINIMAL ENERGY 

SCHEDULES FOR HYBRID DFG I MPLEMENTATIONS 

First, we will prove the existence of periodic schedules for hybrid DFG implementations by proposing a Linear Program (LP) formulation in Section III-A. Then, assuming we want to sustain a given period P , Section III-B shows how to determine a periodic schedule with period P with minimal en-ergy consumption by solving a Mixed-Integer-Linear-Program (MILP) that identifies a set of so-called critical actors . Each actor identified as critical must execute in always-active mode, as otherwise, the induced wake-up or sleep delays would no longer allow for a schedule with the given period P . In contrast, all other actors shall be self-powered.

From the previously introduced examples, we can conclude that there can be quite a huge range of tradeoff solutions between the choice of period P (i.e., throughput) and the achievable energy E of resulting DFG implementations. For performing a Design Space Exploration (DSE) of these trade-offs, we next discuss two alternative brute-force search strate-gies before proposing Hop and Skip , a novel and efficient DSE strategy. For the explanation of the individual strategies, we use as an example a Synchronous Dataflow (SDF) [9] graph called Samplerate from [14]. 2

A. Decision Variable Sweep 

A (true) Pareto front of energy/troughput solutions can be determined by an exhaustive sweep across all 2|A| different combinations of the decision vector x ∈ { 0, 1}|A| (see Lns. 2 to 5 of Alg. 1), and apply the LP from Lst. 1 to deter-mine the minimum sustainable period P (see Ln. 3) and the corresponding energy per period (see Ln. 4) for each configuration. A decision-variable sweep requires solving an LP for each decision vector x, with an LP being solvable in polynomial time. However, the number of configurations grows exponentially in the number of actors |A|. Hence, for large networks, such a strategy is not feasible.

Proof. First, observe that the energy cost function of each actor in Eq. (5) is linear in P , with a positive gradient for both cases of ai being either executed in always-active mode or in self-powered mode (corresponding to pidl (ai) in Eq. (3) and pslp (ai) in Eq. B. Period Sweep

A simple exhaustive search strategy is to sweep the space of all integral periods and determine an energy-minimal implementation for each period simply by solving the MILP as introduced in Lst. 2. The period sweep starts from the minimum period Pmin yielded by the LP with all actors configured to always-active, and ends with the maximum period Pmax , which the LP similarly determines with all actors configured to self-powering mode. C. Hop & Skip DSE Strategy 

The aforementioned period sweep strategy may also require significant computational effort, with Pmax − Pmin + 1 MILP solver runs, underscoring a need for a faster DSE strategy. In the following, we propose an efficient exploration algorithm named Hop and Skip (H&S). Similar to Alg. 2, it starts by determining the period search limits Pmin and Pmax , see Lns. 1 and 2 of the following Alg. 3. These bounds are determined using the LP with all actors configured to always-active (see Ln. 1) and self-powering mode (see Ln. 2), identical to the period bounds as described in Section IV-B. The set of explored points EP is initially cleared (see Ln. 3). Subsequently, the exploration of the design space is started with the period P = Pmax in Ln. 4. The search space of potentially non-dominated candidates is then efficiently traversed from higher values of P to lower values, see the 7

while loop from Lns. 5 to 10, terminating when the period P

is smaller than Pmin .In the loop body, the MILP is called (see Ln. 6) to first determine a decision vector x, corresponding to hopping to a minimum-energy design configuration that sustains the period 

P . For the determined configuration with decision vector x,the minimum achievable period P ′ is then determined by solving the LP (see Ln. 7), potentially reducing the period P

to P ′. This step, similar to skipping over all candidate values from P to P ′, allows for a more efficient traversal over the period space, without requiring to check all integer periods like in Alg. 2, or all 2|A| configurations like in Alg. 1. In Ln. 8, this newly explored point (P ′, E (A, P, x), x) is added to the set of explored points EP . Then, the period P ′ is decremented by a user-defined threshold value ε to determine the next lower P

value in Ln. 9, and the loop is iterated until the end is reached when P < P min . Finally, the set of explored points EP is returned (see Ln. 11).

Experimental Setup and Metrics: Our forthcoming eval-uation consists of: (a) comparing the non-dominated front generated by the period sweep ( P Sweep) and the proposed H&S approach, against the true Pareto front returned by the decision-variable sweep ( x Sweep); and (b) comparing the exploration times of each approach. In general, a Multi-objective Optimization Problem (MOP), in our case, for energy and throughput, does not have a single optimal solution due to multiple conflicting objectives. Instead, there exists a set of Pareto-optimal solutions. The set of all such solutions is known as the Pareto front. The true Pareto front of the MOP that is considered in this paper as a reference can be found by applying the x Sweep strategy (Alg. 1) as was proven in Theorem 1. This set acts as the reference Pareto front and is named SRef in the following. The quality of each approach and each network can then be evaluated by comparing the sets of non-dominated solutions 

SApp of each of the two other DSE strategies introduced in Algorithms 2 and 3 with SRef . For the comparison of explored non-dominated sets of points, we use an indicator known in literature as hypervolume [23]. According to [24], we first normalize the reference Pareto front SRef and each non-dominated front SApp found by P sweep and H&S to only contain objective values between zero and one, i.e., 

SRef , S App ⊂ [0 , 1] d with d = 2 in our case of the two objectives of energy E and period P . Here, an objective value of zero corresponds to the best (i.e., minimal) value ever found for this objective by all approaches for a given application. Conversely, an objective value of one corresponds to the worst (i.e., maximal) value ever encountered. This nor-malization ensures that each objective is weighted equally in the hypervolume quality measure. Then, given a (normalized) Pareto front S ⊂ [0 , 1] d, the hypervolume of S is the measure of the region weakly dominated 3 by S and bounded above by the reference point 1.hypervolume (S) = Λ( {q ∈ [0 , 1] d | ∃ p ∈ S : p ≤ q}) (6) In Eq. (6), Λ( ·) denotes the Lebesgue measure [25]. The greater hypervolume( S) is, the better a Pareto front approxi-mation S is considered to be.

In [26], [27], two different power management strategies are identified that impact scheduling: Dynamic Power Man-agement (DPM) and Dynamic Voltage Scaling (DVS). DPM involves powering down unused modules to save energy, while in DVS, the voltages and frequencies of modules are reduced to fully utilize the idle periods. The reduction in voltages and/or frequencies leads to power and, eventually, energy sav-ings. A detailed overview of DPM is given in [28], while [29] discusses DVS for dataflow-based Systems on Chips (SoCs). Here, the frequencies of individual functional units are varied, subject to throughput constraints, to optimize for energy. In [30], tasks are scheduled to increase the length of the idle period and reduce the number of transitions to and from low-power states to increase energy, using DPM. Moreover, DPM integrates well with the notion of dataflow, as is shown by the concept of self-powering dataflow networks introduced in [7]. For this concept, various ways to fully power down an actor and retend its state are realizable, e.g., utilizing non-volatile memories [8] or even exploiting upcoming FeMFET-based bit cells for fast and local retention of state [22]. Nonetheless, shutdown and wake-up delays may degrade the achievable throughput, necessitating corresponding analysis techniques. Extensive studies on the throughput analysis of DFGs exist in the literature. The maximum cycle mean [11], [12] forms the foundation to analyze the throughput of marked graphs, with further extensions explored in [31] and [32]. Apart from marked graphs, there also exist the SDF [9] and Cyclo-Static Dataflow (CSDF) [33] models of computation, which allow a more concise representation of an application as a DFG, but are of equal computational expressiveness [34] as marked graphs because CSDF and SDF graphs can be translated into marked graphs [15]. In [35], a well-rounded overview of the throughput analysis of SDF graphs is provided, while [36], respectively, [37] provide scheduling algorithms for SDF and CSDF graphs that do not require a translation into marked graphs and, hence, are more computationally efficient than maximum cycle mean-based analysis. None of the previous works is known to us that treats the problem of performance and power tradeoff analysis for data flow specifications in which actors may have varying execution times due to the fact that they might power down in times of idleness due to either clock- or power-gating induced delays. VII. C ONCLUSION 

The introduction of Dynamic Power Management (DPM) strategies such as clock- and power-gating in dataflow net-works has been shown to provide significant energy savings when applied during idle periods. However, these strategies may degrade achievable throughput due to shutdown and wake-up delays. Such throughput degradations might be par-ticularly detrimental to signal processing systems that need to sustain a given throughput. This paper first addresses the fundamental question of whether there exist periodic schedules for dataflow graphs in which, due to data-arrival time dependent activations as a result of shutdown and wake-up delays, the actor execution times may vary. As a result, we show that if for each actor in such a dataflow graph, the decision is taken that an actor either never goes to sleep ( always-active) or always runs in a mode called self-powered , there exists indeed periodic schedules. This is shown by contributing a linear-program formulation for finding a maximal-throughput schedule for a given combination of actors that are exclusively executing in either always-active or self-powered mode in each period (configuration). Moreover, depending on which configuration is chosen, tradeoffs between throughput and energy savings can be an-alyzed. This paper presents three strategies for Design Space Exploration (DSE) of such tradeoffs, including a DSE that explores all configurations which is able to determine all Pareto-optimal configurations, an algorithm that avoids to explore all configurations, but finds for a given period P aconfiguration that has minimal energy E, and finally a very efficient strategy called Hop & Skip (H&S) that is shown to 11 

determine also the Pareto front but skipping to search in period intervals in which no Pareto point may exist. The exploration times as well as the non-dominated sets are compared by the hypervolume. H&S outperforms the other two strategies by speedups of up to three orders of magnitude for real-life and more than 100 generated benchmark applications of different complexity. REFERENCES [1] W.
